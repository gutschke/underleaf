# Pacing — 90 to 120 minutes

## Target budget

The episode targets a **90 to 120 minute session**. Inside that window, the scenes break down as follows:

| Scene | Target time | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Wheels Up | 10 min | Texture only. Don't overrun. |
| 2. The Threads | 15 min | Coincidence pattern. This is the second-most important scene; don't shortchange it. |
| 3. The Hack | 15 min | Plot kicks in. PC3 gets their moment. |
| 4. The Stakes | 10 min | One round of stakes; don't pile on. |
| 5. The Snoop | 15 min | Sneak-comedy. Compressible if the table is flagging. |
| 6. The Bay | 15 min | The dead end. Don't let the bay become heroic. |
| 7. The Gate | 12-15 min | The load-bearing scene. Cannot be compressed below 10 min without losing the ambiguity. |
| 8. Lights Out | 10 min | Quiet close. |
| **Total** | **102-115 min** | Fits the budget. |

Buffer of 5-18 minutes for table chatter, rules questions, bathroom breaks, and the unanticipated.

## What's compressible

If the session is running long:

- **Scene 1** can compress to 5 minutes — fewer vignette beats, less ambient texture.
- **Scene 5** can compress to 10 minutes — skip the bored-passenger sub-plot, give the table a faster pathway to the galley.
- **Scene 8** can compress to 5 minutes — skip some of the disembarkation prose, jump to the empty anti-static bag.

## What's NOT compressible

- **Scene 2 (The Threads)** — the coincidence pattern needs to *land*. If you have only ten minutes, drop a coincidence rather than rushing them; better three landed reveals than five hurried ones.
- **Scene 7 (The Gate)** — needs all twelve minutes minimum. The DM cannot rush the rewind; the ambiguity collapses if the pacing is forced.

## What's expandable

If the table is engaged and the session has the time:

- **Scene 2** can extend to 20-25 minutes if the players are riffing on coincidences.
- **Scene 5** can extend to 25 minutes if the snoop comedy is hitting and the players love improvising covers.
- **Scene 7** can extend to 20 minutes if the players are *in* the gate scene and inventing rich texture.

## Where to cut if the session is short

If you have only 75 minutes:

- Skip Scene 1's vignettes; open in the middle of Scene 2.
- Compress Scene 5 to 8 minutes — fewer obstacles, easier covers.
- Move briskly through Scene 8.

If you have only 60 minutes:

- Don't run this episode. It needs the time it needs. Run a shorter standalone instead and save *Unattended Baggage* for a fuller session.

## Cadence cues

- **Scenes 1-2** are slow, conversational. Voice low; pace deliberate.
- **Scene 3** picks up tempo. PC3 is doing something concrete. The energy shifts.
- **Scene 4** has the highest tension density of the surface plot. Captain's announcements should be read with progressively more performed calm.
- **Scene 5** is comedic. Lean in. Let the table laugh.
- **Scene 6** is quiet again. The bay is small and cold. Let it breathe; let the dead end deflate the table.
- **Scene 7** is slow, deliberate, weird. Drop your voice. Slower.
- **Scene 8** is quiet again. The DM's last words at the table should be measured.

## Mid-session breaks

Don't break in the middle of Scene 2 (kills the coincidence pattern), Scene 6 (kills the deflation), or Scene 7 (kills the ambiguity). Natural break points:

- After Scene 1 (10 min) — if the table is just settling in.
- After Scene 4 (50 min in) — the stakes have just landed; everyone can use a five-minute stretch.
- After Scene 8 (end) — the natural session close.

## A note on real time vs. play time

The session is roughly 90-120 minutes of real time. The story spans about 18 hours of in-fiction time:

- Three hours of preceding gate / boarding (relived in Scene 7).
- Six hours of flight before the cold open.
- Eight hours of flight during the episode (Scenes 1-7 occur over ~3-4 hours of cabin time).
- Final ~5 hours to landing (compressed in Scene 8).

Players will not notice the time-compression unless they're tracking it. The DM can mention it in passing if needed (*"a couple of hours pass"*), but the real-time cadence is the one that matters.
