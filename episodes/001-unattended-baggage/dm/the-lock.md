# The lock — an optional coincidence

In one real-play run of Episode 1, a small thing happened that nobody planned. A PC was given the combination to *another* PC's checked luggage (to fetch an eye mask). There is no luggage access from the lower deck — but the **same combination** opened the secured hatch into the engineering space. The table barely noticed. It is one of the best cheap-tier specimens of how this world actually works (see [`the-cable.md`](the-cable.md) and [`world-truths.md`](../../../design/DM-ONLY/world-truths.md): loose causality is *cheap*; the world routes a structural need through the cheapest unremarkable path), and it pays off beautifully in Episode 2.

This file lets a DM running Episode 1 fresh **make that coincidence likely to recur** — without authoring it, and without losing anything if it doesn't fire. It is the cheap-tier sibling of the gate scene: same "deny nothing, confirm nothing" discipline, but you never have to rewind time.

## The principle

Don't place the coincidence. Place *both loose halves* and let the table's own behaviour snap them together:

- **(A)** a PC ends up holding a 3- or 4-digit combination from an unrelated, in-fiction-legitimate source, said *out loud at the table*; and
- **(B)** the secured hatch into the E&E space carries a combination lock whose code you quietly set **equal to that spoken number**.

You author both halves loosely. The match is the players' doing. That is what keeps it from ever feeling planted.

## Where it attaches

- **The seed** plants in **Scene 4 (The Stakes)** or early **Scene 5 (The Snoop)** — an in-character errand that gets a number spoken aloud.
- **The collision** lands in **Scene 6 (The Bay)** — a secured inner hatch at the bottom of the ladder, before the bay read-aloud. (See the optional note now in [`../scenes/06-the-bay.md`](../scenes/06-the-bay.md).)

## Three plants (so at least one can fire — none is forced)

**Plant 1 — the luggage-combo errand (primary; this is what happened in play).**
Give an erratic / pushy PC a reason to send someone to their checked bag. During The Stakes, as ambient texture:

> *"You sleep with an eye mask — and yours is in your checked bag, not your carry-on. Seatbelt sign's been on a while. You're not getting up."*

They'll conscript someone. When they do, ask the natural question:

> *"What's the combination? They'll need it for the bag."*

The player says a number **out loud**. Write it down — flag it `LOCK-COMBO = ____`. The errand itself *fails* (there is no lower-deck luggage access; see [`avionics-realism.md`](avionics-realism.md)) — which is perfect. The number has now been spoken and served no purpose: loose, purposeless causality, exactly what the Quiet routes through.

**Plant 2 — a mundane code (backup, if no one asks for a luggage combo).**
Get a number spoken aloud some other ordinary way during the snoop: a PC sets a temporary TSA-lock code while repacking ("what'd you set it to?"); a crew supply drawer has a code on a label a PC reads aloud; the hacker PC reads a service-panel code off a bulkhead.

**Plant 3 — no number is produced.** Then the coincidence simply isn't happening this run. Fine (see Fallback).

## The collision (Scene 6, before the bay read-aloud)

At the bottom of the ladder, narrate a secured inner hatch with a four-wheel mechanical combination lock — *boring hardware, the gym-locker kind.* Set its code equal to a spoken number, and **say nothing about that.** Then **wait. Do not prompt.** The whole magic of the real-play beat was that the table closed the loop themselves ("...wait, try the luggage number"). If a player offers the number, the wheels turn and it opens, **first try.** Narrate it as nothing — one detail among many, straight into the bay read-aloud:

> *"The wheels click into the detent. The shackle pops. Cold air rises through the gap."*

## The fallback (loses nothing)

- **No number spoken, or nobody connects it** → the tech/Subterfuge PC picks it, or it was left hanging open by the last mechanic. Five-second obstacle. The coincidence just didn't manifest at this table — per the engagement-layer guarantee, that costs the episode nothing.
- **A number was spoken but nobody tries it** → do **not** rescue it. Never have an NPC suggest the code. Let it pass. A missed cheap coincidence is invisible; a forced one is authored.
- **Digit-count mismatch** (luggage was 3 wheels, hatch is 4) → only if the table is *reaching* for it: "the first three match what you'd expect; the fourth you guess in a try or two." Bias toward the table's own momentum; never toward inserting it.

## Keeping it deniable

This is easier than the gate scene — nobody has to rewind time, and the cover is built in: a four-wheel lock has 10,000 combinations, and *somebody's* luggage code matching *some* hatch code is the kind of thing that happens to someone, somewhere, constantly. Coincidence is a complete, honest explanation; you never have to lie.

- **Never react to the match.** Same vocal energy as a galley curtain sliding aside. No "huh," no pause, no eyebrow.
- **If a player flags it in the moment** ("wait, that's *my* bag's combo"): a shrug, not a poker face — *"Huh. Long flight."* — and into the bay. Do **not** escalate to the gate scene's *"could be, could not be."* That register is for the connector (an expensive, Hard-tier event); over-weighting a cheap coincidence tips your hand. For a cheap coincidence, the shrug *is* the poker face.
- **Write the spoken number into your session notes verbatim.** You need it for Episode 2.

## Why it matters later

If it fires, it becomes a load-bearing *cheap-end* specimen on the campaign's cost-gradient, and Episode 2 ("The Archivist's Index") is built to resurface it — see that episode's [`dm/the-lock-callback.md`](../../002-the-archivists-index/dm/the-lock-callback.md). If it does **not** fire, Episode 2 simply skips those beats; they are all conditional. Nothing breaks either way.
