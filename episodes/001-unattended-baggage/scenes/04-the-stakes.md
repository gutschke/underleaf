# The Stakes

Twenty minutes after Scene 3, the cabin PA crackles.

## DM read-aloud (the announcement)

Read this in the calm, slightly-too-friendly voice of a long-haul airline captain:

> *"Folks, this is the flight deck. We're showing a minor electrical fault that the crew here wants to have looked at on the ground. Nothing affecting your safety or comfort — we just want to be thorough. We'll be making a brief unscheduled stop at Narita in about three hours. Once we're on the ground, the airline will provide updates on your continuing service to Taipei. We anticipate a delay of approximately eight hours. We'll update you with more information as we have it. Thank you for your patience."*

## What happens next

Each PC's personal stake (chosen during prep from [`dm/stakes.md`](../dm/stakes.md)) hits. The DM walks the table through this in order, giving each PC a moment.

- The PC whose **cousin's premature baby** is hospitalized: eight hours could be the difference. The cousin's partner is alone in a Taipei NICU. The PC was supposed to land at 06:00 local. Eight more hours stretches that to 14:00. They are not religious but they have been saying small things in the air for the last hour.
- The PC with the **wedding in forty-eight hours**: rehearsal dinner is tomorrow night. They are the best man / maid of honor / officiant. There is no rebooking that gives them time to recover. They have not slept properly in a week.
- The PC with the **parent moving to hospice tomorrow**: they are flying to be there for the move-in. They had wanted to say something specific. They have rehearsed it. They will lose the chance if they're not there.
- The PC with the **visa appointment**: it is non-rebookable for six months. Their work depends on it.
- The PC with **the forty-hour wake**: they were *barely* holding it together to get on this flight. Eight more hours of airport limbo plus another flight is real, possibly clinical, sleep deprivation. They are not at their best.

After the round of stakes, one PC says it aloud or someone realizes it silently: *we have to do something*.

## The PA returns

In Scene 4's middle, the captain comes back on:

> *"Folks, an update. Our maintenance partners in Tokyo are advising that the fix may take longer than initially estimated. We're now looking at a possible delay of twelve hours. We will continue to update you as we have more information. Thank you."*

Twelve. Then thirteen. Then "we'll update you when we know more." The eight-hour estimate has *quietly* become an open-ended one.

## DM cue

By the end of this scene, the PCs should be deciding to investigate. The DM doesn't push — the personal stakes do the work. But if the table needs a nudge:

> *"{{pc:1}}, you find yourself looking at {{pc:3}}'s laptop. Then at the panel that says 'Crew Only' beside the forward galley. Then back at the laptop."*

That is enough. The decision crystallizes. Move into Scene 5.

## What this scene is doing

Converting a nuisance delay into a personal emergency for five different people. Authorizing the next twenty minutes of risk-taking.

## A note for the DM

Don't make the stakes feel manipulative. Each PC's stake should be presented straight, given a moment, not piled on. The cumulative weight is the point; piling on each individual stake will make the scene feel forced.

Read the captain's announcements at slightly different speeds. The first is calm. The second is calm but the calm is starting to feel performed.
