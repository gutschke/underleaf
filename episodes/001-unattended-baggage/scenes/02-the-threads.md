# The Threads

The conversation finds itself.

## DM read-aloud

> *Once two or three of you are talking, the others drift in. A long flight makes strangers into temporary neighbors; the cabin is small and the night is long. Within twenty minutes, four of you are seated in a loose half-circle, drinks in hand, the lights still low. The fifth — wherever they are — is at most a row away and can hear.*

## What happens at the table

The DM steers conversation toward backstory in the most natural way possible — *"so what's taking you to Taipei?"* — and lets the players answer for their characters. After the easy answers (work, family, vacation), the DM starts seeding coincidences from the menu in [`dm/coincidences.md`](../dm/coincidences.md). Pick three or four that fit your PCs' backstories.

Surface them in passing. Not "you all grew up on the same street," but:

> *"Wait — Outer Sunset? Which block?"*
> *"Twenty-second and Judah."*
> *"…I lived on Twentieth from age four to twelve."*

Let the realization settle on the table by itself. The PCs notice their reactions noticing themselves. By the third coincidence, someone should ask half-jokingly: *"Okay, what's going on here?"*

The DM does not answer. The DM lets the question sit.

## What also happens (Taipei reasons)

Each PC explains, in the same conversation, why they're on this specific flight. The DM has assigned each player a personal stake from [`dm/stakes.md`](../dm/stakes.md) during prep. At least one of those reasons should be visibly *last-minute* — the kind of trip you would not have predicted a week ago.

- "My cousin's baby was born premature; they need help."
- "A wedding I can't miss."
- "My parent is being moved to hospice tomorrow."
- "A visa appointment that took eight months to book."
- "I've been awake forty hours straight and I needed to be on this plane."

The combination of mid-conversation coincidences plus visibly-recent travel decisions is where the table's intuition starts to tilt.

## Optional moment

If the table is engaged and the conversation is flowing, the DM can drop one passing detail with no follow-up:

> *"While you're talking, one of the cabin crew passes through with the meal-service tray. PC1, she meets your eye for a half-second longer than is comfortable, then looks past you."*

Do not return to this beat. Do not call attention to it. Players who notice will remember; players who don't will not. Either is fine.

## What this scene is doing

Setting up the *coincidence pattern* the campaign rides for fifty sessions. The PCs are starting to feel watched — but by something benevolent and bumbling, not malicious. They have no framework yet.

The DM keeps the conversational rhythm light. The eerie part is the players' own reactions; the DM does not need to add tension.

## Transitions

This scene ends when the conversation naturally cools, the cabin lights stay dim, and the PCs settle back into their own seats. Scene 3 begins when PC3 (the SDR / radio-hobbyist PC) starts paying attention to their laptop with new alertness.

If you want a transition beat: the cabin's PA system produces a single small click — not an announcement, just the sound of the channel opening and closing. Move into Scene 3.
