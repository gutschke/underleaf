# The lock callback — forcing the coincidence back into the plot (gently)

**Hard gate: every beat here is conditional on the combination-lock coincidence having FIRED in Episode 1** (see [`../../001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-lock.md`](../../001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-lock.md)). If it didn't fire at your table, skip this file entirely — there's nothing to resurface.

The goal (the user's instinct, and it's correct): a single cheap coincidence, never re-touched, decays from memory within a session. Players will forget it. We bring it back — *briefly* — so it can become part of the pattern. The discipline that keeps "bringing it back" from becoming "railroading their attention": **re-stage the conditions under which the lock matters, and let a player retrieve it. Never deliver it yourself.** A coincidence a player *notices* is a chill; one they're *shown* is homework.

And the load-bearing rule: **be genuinely fine if they miss it.** The callback you'd be okay with them missing is the only kind that delivers a chill when they catch it. (If it's missed this session, it can resurface in sessions 3–5; nothing is lost.)

> **Calibration (the headline rule — read this first).** Run **one emphasized** path (Bea's recategorization), **one light** path (the cold-open primer), and **one player-pulled** path (the maintenance paperwork). Do **not** run all three at full volume — that tips it from "the world has a habit" to "the DM is running a motif." If you only do one thing, do Bea.
>
> **You don't need the verbatim combination for any of this.** The *event* resurfaces, not the digits. Even if you didn't log the exact number in Episode 1, every beat below still works (the decoys don't use it; the primer rides the player's own recall).

## Three re-entry paths (the table needs to catch only one)

Independent paths, so it can't be wholly missed — but graded so only one is *emphasized*. Don't run all three at full volume; that tips from "the world has a habit" to "the DM is running a motif."

### 1. The Bea recategorization (PRIMARY — the load-bearing one)

Attach: Scene 6, Bea's index. This is the strongest because number-coincidences are *native* to Bea's files — no contrivance. She reads a few **decoy "lucky number" entries** — strangers' events that rhyme with the bay-hatch lock without being it — as examples of her taxonomy:

> *(flipping cards)* *"People bring me the strangest ones. A man's garage code opened his new landlord's storage unit across town — different building, same four digits. A kid's bike-lock combination was the same as the school rack. A woman's hotel safe opened on her dead mother's birthday, which she'd never set. They think it means something. Mostly it means there's ten thousand of those little numbers and a lot of locks."*

Buried in that list, the PCs' own lock **self-selects** — *their private weirdness was never unique; it's a type Bea has a whole drawer of.* That recategorization is what makes it finally **stick**: you're not reminding them it happened, you're telling them it has *company* and a name-shaped drawer. A phenomenon with a category sticks; a one-off doesn't.

- **Surface:** Bea is a charming eccentric with trivia. Registers nothing more. Fine.
- **Middle:** "...that garage thing is exactly like the bay door." The connection lands; they may or may not voice it.
- **Deep:** asks Bea which ones *weren't* nothing. She won't say (*"I keep the records..."*).

If the table doesn't react to the first decoy, she has a second card — keep it a clear *category-mate, not a near-replica* of the PCs' event (a padlock match on a storage cage, a boat-locker, a self-storage unit — combination coincidences, but not an aircraft/secured-door shape). Two casts of the bait inside one scene; then stop.

### 2. The cold-open primer (LIGHT — keeps the digits warm)

Attach: Scene 1. The fifteen-second *mundane* locker beat for the PC who held/used the combo (sample phrasing in [`../scenes/01-reconvene.md`](../scenes/01-reconvene.md)). Keep it **inert** — no eerie hesitation, no "the number feels like it belongs to something else." That coloring is the DM pointing; it broadcasts significance before the connection is earned. The beat just surfaces the digits neutrally ("the same four digits, by habit") to keep them warm so path 1 can do the connecting work. Say it once, doing another job, no pause, no glance. Floor, not ceiling.

### 3. The maintenance-paperwork branch (OPTIONAL — player-initiated only)

Attach: Scene 4, the footage. **Only if a PC digs the maintenance paperwork** alongside the camera (the hacker's instinct). They turn up the aircraft's turnaround sheet for that night; among the routine entries, a line in a mechanic's handwriting:

> *"Fwd E&E access — opened for inspection 0214, re-secured. No discrepancy."*

No forced entry, no damage write-up, no work order explaining *why* it was opened mid-turnaround. It was opened by someone who simply… opened it — i.e. someone with the code, not someone who broke in (a pried or drilled lock leaves a damage note; there is none). The paperwork corroborates that the hatch came open that night and confirms nothing about how. It dead-ends like everything else: a handwritten note, an off-book open, no one left to ask.

This is a real *positive find* for the hacker (a found-by-skill fact, even if it explains nothing), and it sits on the cost-gradient as the *cheap, loose* trace (a human note, weakly anchored) beside the connector's *expensive, occluded* one.

> **Do NOT** use an ACARS / electronic "tamper log" / "first attempt, no retries" version. The Episode-1 hatch is a *mechanical* gym-locker lock (see [`../../001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-lock.md`](../../001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-lock.md)) — no sensor, can't log attempts — and an instrumented record would mis-file the lock as an *expensive anchor* instead of the cheap loose trace it is. Keep the corroboration mundane and on paper.

**Never volunteer this** — it appears only if a player reaches for it. (If the hacker isn't on the footage, route it to another Tech/Subterfuge PC, or let it be a printout in Bea's files.)

## Who raises it — bait, don't deliver

Priority: a PC (baited) > evidence/Bea > DM narrator (last resort, basically never).

- **Safe out-loud bait — the caregiver/curious PC.** Agenda-free; can't hijack, won't escalate. If you want it said aloud, the Bea decoys are most likely to make this player go "wait, isn't that like—".
- **High-resonance private bait — the Outsider (memory-loss backstory).** Don't bait them to *speak*; bait them to *feel*. The records-say-no / memory-says-yes shape is their whole life. Let them connect it privately; a quiet player carrying it internally is the best version.
- **Deny ownership to the Control-seeker.** Don't bait them to raise the lock; if they grab it, it becomes *their* theory to control and steps on the Outsider's earned moment. Feed their control-need with Bea-as-Influence-target instead. If they do voice it, neutral non-co-sign ("could be") and hand action to another PC so it doesn't crystallize as their canon.
  - **Watch the follower-bond collision.** If the Control-seeker has a follower-style bond with a quieter PC (as in the example party — see [`../../../characters/pcs/example-party.md`](../../../characters/pcs/example-party.md) — where the follower may well be the Outsider you're protecting), they may turn to that PC and narrate *for* them ("you felt it too — tell them what you saw"), annexing the Outsider's private chill through the established control relationship. **Bounce straight to the Outsider** ("that's *you* — what do *you* notice?") and, when you can, deliver their resonance as a **private DM↔player aside** (lean in, low voice) so it lands out of the Control-seeker's earshot. The Outsider's connection is a DM↔player channel, not a table-public beat the Control-seeker can seize.
- **Aim the armed PC's frustration at the cover-up,** not the coincidence or you.

## When a player connects it — do NOT co-sign

Treat a correct connection exactly like a wrong one: a beat of nothing, then bounce. *"Could be. Could not be."* — then to another PC or the surface task. The realization beat is sessions 3–5; confirming in session 2 spends the payoff early and converts a spooky coincidence (interesting) into a confirmed clue (less interesting). The chill is the player's; if you grab it, you've taken it.

For a *cheap* coincidence specifically, even "could be, could not be" can over-weight it — a shrug is often better: *"Long flight does funny things."* Read the room.

## Do NOT attach the lock to the closing accidental cast (Scene 7)

Deliberate omission. Tying the lock to the cast implies the lock *was* a cast (violates "never confirm as magic") and front-runs the realization arc. Keep the lock in the **coincidence register** (cold open / Bea / footage). The lock and the cast converge later — in sessions 3–5 — when the players themselves assemble "the too-lucky events were all the same thing." Let them make that connection; don't make it for them.

## Traps

- **Forced-nudge tells (never do):** lingering after the number, repeating it, glancing at the baited player, letting silence hang for the catch, an NPC saying "funny, numbers do that around here." All broadcast "this matters" and kill the chill.
- **Over-touching:** three full-volume beats reads as a DM motif. One emphasized (Bea) + one light (cold open) + one player-pulled (log) is the calibration. Don't add a fourth.
- **Ambiguity fatigue:** this session already stacks non-answers (denials, inconclusive footage). Pair the ambiguity with the concrete per-session beat (Scene 7's cast that worked) so the table gets a *win* and the ambiguity reads as depth, not stonewalling.
