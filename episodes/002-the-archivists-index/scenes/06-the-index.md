# The Index

The centerpiece. Full scene handling, Bea's refusal script, the 1987 card, and the lock recategorization are in [`../dm/the-archivist.md`](../dm/the-archivist.md) — read it before running this. Bea's sheet: [`../../../characters/npcs/bea-ferro.json`](../../../characters/npcs/bea-ferro.json).

## The house

A fog-grey house near Ocean Beach. Bea is early seventies, warm and dry, reading glasses on a chain. She apologizes for the clutter and doesn't mean it. The clutter is filing boxes, decades of them.

> *"You're the ones who called about the airport. Come in, mind the boxes. Tea? I only have the one kind."*

## The wall of clippings

She has spent forty years cataloguing improbable losses and improbable saves — the claims nobody else wanted, the "acts of God." She'll show the PCs her method: cards, clippings, a taxonomy of luck. Decades of the same fingerprint — an impossible outcome paired with a record or a witness that doesn't match.

## The refusal (hard invariant)

Bea **will not explain it.** She shows records; she never draws the conclusion.

> *"I keep the records. I don't explain them. You'll understand or you won't — that's the only honest way to do this. People who got explained to stopped looking. I'd rather you looked."*

Her reason (if pressed): she was called a crank her whole career; she's done being disbelieved; being *believed* matters to her more than being *understood*. If a deep-layer player corners her, hold the same off-the-record discipline as the gate scene (see [`../../001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-gate.md`](../../001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-gate.md)), in Bea's own words: *"I want you to find that out for yourselves, not from me."*

If a control-seeking PC works her socially, let that be their lane — she's warm, she'll give them tea and time — but **she does not crack.** Her refusal is characterful and final, not a stonewall.

## The 1987 card (the thesis statement)

She pulls one card without being asked, and lets them read it while she watches their faces:

> *A child pulled from a flooded culvert outside Pacifica in the winter of 1987 by a man no one could afterward identify. The responding deputy's logbook has a clean forty-minute gap. The child lived. Nobody ever found the man.*

Same fingerprint as the gate: an impossible save, an erased record. (Note the *vanished rescuer* — a human agent the gate edit doesn't have. That's a feature: it feeds the "individual benefactor" misread the PCs are *supposed* to adopt and later have dissolved. It does not name a culprit for the gate edit; it's a different event with its own unfindable agent.) *This is old. It is a pattern. The erasure is part of it.* The belief this episode revises lands here: **not "this happened to us, uniquely" but "this has happened to others for decades, and someone has been quietly keeping the files."**

## The lock recategorization (if the lock fired in Episode 1)

Among her example cards, she reads a few **decoy "lucky number" coincidences** — strangers' events that *rhyme* with the PCs' bay-hatch lock without being it (a garage code that opened a stranger's storage unit across town; a bike-lock combination that matched the school rack). Buried in the list, the PCs' own lock self-selects: *their private weirdness was never unique — it's a type Bea has a whole drawer of.* That recategorization is what makes the lock finally **stick.** Full handling and sample phrasings: [`../dm/the-lock-callback.md`](../dm/the-lock-callback.md). Skip if the lock never fired.

## Optional seed (1 minute)

A clipping in her files about **Dr. Reuben Marsh** and his galaxies-are-sentient hypothesis — a misdirection seed-bank plant that pays off much later. The Lore Reader catches it; nobody else needs to.

## Priority if time is short

Bea carries a lot in 20 minutes. The **load-bearing trio** is: (1) the scale / being-believed beat, (2) the 1987 card, (3) the network-exists-but-unnamed bridge. The **lock recategorization** and the **Marsh seed** are explicitly *droppable* — the lock resurfaces fine in sessions 3–5, and Marsh is pure Lore-Reader bonus. Don't let Bea become an info-faucet by volume; if the table engages deeply on the trio, cut 4 and 5.

## What this scene is doing

Widening the mystery from a personal one-off to a decades-deep, already-catalogued phenomenon (the episode's belief-revision); introducing Bea as a recurring, withholding figure and the bridge to the network (Mira, later); making the lock stick by giving it a category; and delivering the vertigo of scale + the relief of being *believed*.

## Pacing

~20 minutes; the longest scene. Don't let it become an exposition dump — Bea answers with cards, not lectures. If the table is hungry for more, she shows another card, never an explanation.
