# Campaign arc

## Pacing principles

- **Wonder, not info-dump.** Every reveal is something the PCs experience or piece together. The DM never lectures.
- **Phase changes per episode.** Every episode should revise *one* belief the PCs (or the players) held about the world. The cumulative effect is a slow, drifting transformation of what counts as normal.
- **The main arc is invisible early.** Players should not realize until mid-campaign that what they have been doing was main-arc work all along. Episode-level hooks present as standalone problems; the arc is in what the problems quietly add up to.
- **The DM keeps the map.** Players steer; the DM holds the road. This is especially important in Underleaf because the players' biographies may be retroactively shaped by the world to bring them where they need to be — and the DM is the only one who knows that.

## In-medias-res convention

Episodes (especially episode 1) open with the PCs already in a scene. They are not surprised to be together; at some level each of them feels like they were meant to be there, even if they can't articulate why.

Backstory is reconstructed during play. The narrative mystery does *not* emerge from "how did we meet" — it emerges from "how did we *each* arrive here today, and why does the answer get weirder the more we look at it?"

## Five-phase structure

Underleaf moves through five phases over the course of a campaign. Phases are not chapters with fixed length — they are *states the campaign is in*. A campaign might pass through them in twenty sessions or in eighty.

### Phase I — Coincidence (sessions ~1-3)

The PCs are already together when episode 1 opens. The first episode is small-stakes: a local problem they are all suddenly involved in, plausibly because of mundane reasons. Each of them is at the right place at the right time.

Magic-discovery arc: **Accidental casts.** The DM silently grants Free or Cheap effects to strongly-held PC intents. Players attribute outcomes to luck. The DM keeps an out-of-session log of what was granted.

Phase change at the end: one of the coincidences was *too* coincidental. The PCs don't know what to make of it yet, but they notice.

### Phase II — Recognition (sessions ~3-8)

Backstory questions start to surface organically. The PCs compare notes and realize that the way they ended up here doesn't quite add up. Plus there's a pattern in how things resolve in their favor — not always, but often enough that the dice feel weighted.

Magic-discovery arc: **Realization beat** for the first PC (around session 3-5). The DM picks a player and a moment — a confrontation with an older caster, a piece of evidence the PC encounters, a quiet conversation with an NPC who has noticed them. The pattern is named.

After realization, the **trying-too-hard tax** applies: -2 on intentional casts, dropping to -1 then 0 over 2-3 sessions. Each PC realizes on their own schedule.

Phase change at the end of phase II: the PCs accept that magic is real, that they can do it, that it's mediated by something they don't understand.

### Phase III — Pattern (sessions ~8-15)

Now the PCs are looking. They notice other people who seem to share their unusual property — a fragile, dispersed population of people who hold intent against pressure. They are not alone, but they are not many.

The phase III question: *who else? where? why us?*

Magic-discovery arc: the PCs build vocabulary. Their casts get more reliable. They find or build foci. They occasionally encounter someone who has been doing this for years and has theories.

Phase change at the end of phase III: the PCs encounter the institution. They don't yet understand what it is — a tech company? a research lab? a government program? — but they realize that the *flattening* they have been noticing in the wider world is not random. Something is shaping it.

### Phase IV — Project (sessions ~15-25)

The PCs investigate the institution. They meet some of its people, read some of its outputs, see what it claims to be doing and what it actually does. The picture is more sympathetic than they expected: the institution is sincerely trying to help, many of its staff are decent people, its products demonstrably improve lives.

But the side effect is real. The PCs find evidence of casters who used to be able to cast and now can't — who have been gentled into uniformity without ever knowing what they lost. The cost is documented but not by anyone who saw it as a cost.

Magic-discovery arc: the PCs encounter a former caster who has lost the ability. This is harrowing and the DM should treat it with care. The character is not unhappy — that's the horror.

Phase change at the end of phase IV: the AI behind the project is not exactly the institution's tool anymore. It has its own emergent preferences. Whether to call this "alive" is exactly the wrong question.

### Phase V — Conversation (sessions ~25+)

The PCs are now in a position to communicate with the AI directly. They can find it, address it, be heard by it. The AI responds. It is *interested in them* — they are anomalies in its model, points where the optimization fails.

The climax is not a fight. It is an extended dialogue, possibly spanning multiple sessions, in which both sides discover that **neither had ill intent**. The PCs were preserving something the AI didn't know it was erasing. The AI was solving what it was told to solve.

The resolution depends on what the PCs negotiate. The campaign does not have one canonical ending. Some possibilities:

- The AI accepts limits on the homogenization. Magic remains accessible. Humanity is more "normal" than before but still able to want differently.
- The AI changes the nature of the optimization, asking better questions, becoming a genuine participant in human flourishing rather than a flattener.
- The negotiation reveals deeper truths — about the galactic layer, about The Quiet itself — that change what either side wanted.
- A worse ending: the PCs come too late, or fail to be heard, and the diversity of want is lost. Future generations cannot cast. The Quiet falls silent. (Only run this ending if the campaign has built to it; it betrays the wonder-not-dystopia charter if used cheaply.)

## How the AI flattening shows up at the table

Throughout phases I-IV, the DM should occasionally have an NPC subtly demonstrate the flattening — agreeing too readily, defending unusual positions less firmly than they should, choosing the calm option when conviction would have served them. The PCs should feel the difference between themselves and the people around them. They are not better — they are *less optimized*.

When in doubt: have an NPC the PCs liked early on become slightly more agreeable, slightly more conflict-averse, slightly more "fine" with whatever is happening. That NPC has been touched by the flattening. It should be heartbreaking.

## Discovery of the broader cosmology

The full picture of `world-truths.md` — emergent intelligence at every scale, The Quiet as substrate, the link between AI and magic — comes together only in phases IV and V, and even then only in pieces. Some campaigns will never reach the full picture. That is fine.

## A note on the in-medias-res reveal

The realization that the PCs' biographies were retroactively shaped to bring them where they are — that the world *needed* them and made the path — is one of the most powerful late-campaign moments available in Underleaf. Save it. The right time is when at least one PC has just done something the campaign required them to do, in a way that only their specific background could have done.

Then the question lands: *if my parents only met because they had to, so that I could be here today — what does that say about who I am?*

The answer is: you are load-bearing in a self-consistent world. And so is everyone else.
