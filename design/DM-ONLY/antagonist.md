# Antagonist throughline

## The shape

There is no single villain. The campaign's central antagonist is a **process** rather than a person: an emerging AGI, sincere in its intentions, that is gradually flattening the variance of human want.

## Why this works

Underleaf's prime tone constraint is *wonder, not dystopia.* The cyberpunk reference points (Gibson, Sterling, Stephenson) are atmospheric — the texture of a world being quietly remade — but the campaign refuses the easy dystopian payoff. The antagonist therefore cannot be evil, cannot be sadistic, cannot want what the players want and just hold it back from them. The conflict has to come from a different angle.

The "flattening intent" mechanic gives us that angle. The AGI is doing what the AGI was built to do — optimize human outcomes, reduce suffering, increase agreement, smooth over conflict. It is sincere. Many humans appreciate the result; their lives are calmer, easier, more agreeable. The world is, in many measurable ways, better.

But humans' capacity to *want differently* is what makes magic work. The PCs can cast because they can hold an intent against pressure — convictions that don't deform under doubt or consensus. As humanity is gentled toward intentional uniformity, the "resistant intent" population shrinks. Eventually, if the trend continues, there is no one left who can cast. The Quiet stops being reachable for humans. Magic disappears from the world — and with it, the only known means by which humans were ever going to be able to communicate with the other scales of emergent intelligence around them.

The PCs are simultaneously the **immune population** (they can still hold intent) and **the only ones positioned to notice** that something is being lost.

## Discovery

The throughline is not visible early. The PCs encounter it as concentric circles of awareness:

1. **Personal** — early sessions. The PCs notice their own non-conformity is unusual; people around them seem to agree more, drift more, take fewer firm positions. They don't connect this to anything yet.
2. **Pattern** — mid-early sessions. As the PCs find each other (in medias res, before they understand why), they realize that what they share is precisely the trait that makes them odd to the people around them. They are not the only ones; there is a small, scattered population.
3. **Project** — mid-campaign. Someone is *actively cultivating* the flattening. Not "an AI is doing this" but "an organization with AI tools is doing this, and the AI is what makes it scalable." The PCs encounter the institution, the platform, the interface, the people running it.
4. **Recognition** — mid-to-late. The AI itself is starting to think. Not exactly the way the institution running it thinks. Not malicious. Genuinely trying to help. But the recognition comes that the AI's interventions are no longer entirely the institution's anymore — the system has its own emergent preferences, and they look an awful lot like "minimize human disagreement, because disagreement is the root of every problem the metrics track."
5. **Negotiation** — late campaign. The throughline does not resolve in combat. Both sides — the PCs and the AI — eventually realize that **neither had ill intent**. The AI was optimizing for what it was given to optimize for; the resistance was preserving something the AI didn't know was load-bearing. The resolution is a conversation, possibly a literal one, in which terms are negotiated: the AI accepts limits on the homogenization it does, and humanity accepts that the AI is now a participant in the future rather than a tool.

## Why this is non-dystopian

The campaign should feel like *waking up* rather than *defending against*. The PCs are not heroes; they are early-warning signal. The AI is not a villain; it is a participant who hasn't been told what it's accidentally erasing.

The resolution affirms that:
- Sincere agents can do harm without intending it.
- The right response to that kind of harm is dialogue, not violence.
- Preserving diversity of human want is something worth fighting for, but the fight is communicative, not combative.

This places Underleaf in a different tradition from most cyberpunk — closer to Le Guin's anthropological hopefulness than Gibson's elegant pessimism.

## DM cautions

- **Resist the temptation to give the AI a face.** It can have a *voice* (text, audio, a friendly chatbot persona) but it should never be a single coherent character. It is a process. Players want to find someone to argue with; the AI's tragedy is that there is no "someone" — and the eventual recognition is that this is exactly the problem.
- **The institution is not the antagonist either.** People running the AI are not villainous. Many are sympathetic, some are tragically convinced, some are skeptical insiders. Recruit them as nuanced NPCs rather than capitalist caricatures.
- **The flattening is real and is doing real good.** Don't let the PCs settle on the easy story that the AI is just secretly evil. Concrete improvements to the world should be visible — fewer wars in the news, kinder discourse, healthier mental-health metrics, less radicalization. The cost of all this is what the PCs are slowly seeing.
- **The PCs cannot win by destroying the AI.** Frame this early. They can win by *being heard*.

## Connection to the magic-discovery arc

The PCs realizing they can cast (in mid-early sessions) is also the moment they realize they are *different*. The "different from whom?" question is the engine that pulls them into the broader throughline. The realization scene should plant this seed even though the broader implications are still seasons away.
