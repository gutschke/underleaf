# Underleaf design principles

These are the load-bearing authorial principles for Underleaf. They apply across every episode, every NPC, every scene the DM writes or improvises. They were locked 2026-05-20.

## 1. Possible future, not prophecy

**Use the players' real-life Bay Area as the campaign's setting. Make the PCs different people.**

The original Underleaf run uses the San Francisco Bay Area because that's where the human players live. Real neighborhoods, real freeway exits, real coffee shops, real airports. When the cabin lights dim on Flight 887 and the captain announces a stop at Narita, the route geography is real; players can look it up and verify.

**But the PCs are not the players.** A PC has a *different* name, gender, identity. A PC living in the Outer Sunset is not the player who lives in the Outer Sunset. Same neighborhood; different person.

### Why this works

- The eeriness lands harder when the location is home. *"You're at the gate at SFO Terminal G"* hits differently when the player has stood in that exact spot.
- Players are not personally implicated when bad things happen to their PC. Underleaf does not threaten its players' lives with prophecy.
- The campaign reads as a *possible* future for our shared world, not a *prediction* about anyone at the table.

### When forking to a different city

If you fork Underleaf and relocate it to your city — Boston, Berlin, Portland, Lagos — keep the same principle. Real locale. Fictional identities. Players play people who live in the city you all share, but the people are not the players.

### When this principle gets tested

A player will sometimes wander toward making their PC autobiographical. *"Actually my character's job is the same as my real job."* The DM should not stop this gently, but should ask one question: *"What's different about them from you?"* Find one substantial difference. That's enough. The player keeps the texture they want; the PC stays a distinct person.

## 2. Engagement layers

**Three depths. All three are valid. None is required.**

Underleaf has cosmology that includes The Quiet, emergent intelligence at every scale, retrocausality, and an AGI-flattens-intent throughline. Some players will love this stuff and want to chase every thread. Other players will not be interested in cosmology at all. **Both kinds of player are welcome and the table should accommodate both.**

### Surface layer

The immediate drama: the plane is in trouble, find the part, talk to the gate agent, save the relationship, win the argument, get the medicine to the patient. A player engaged at the surface layer is having a great time. They make plans, react to NPCs, roll dice, laugh at the cover-story improv. They never have to ask *"what was that scene?"* and they never have to think about The Quiet.

### Middle layer

Noticing patterns. The eerie coincidences. The "huh, that's weird" reflex. *"Wait, didn't that happen to me last month too?"* A middle-layer player asks questions about the world but doesn't necessarily chase the answers. They like the texture of the unexplained.

### Deep layer

Speculation about *why*. The Quiet, the AGI, the sub-Planck cosmology, the implications for free will, the long arc of the campaign. A deep-layer player will hypothesize, theorize, and chase the implications. They will want to know if other casters exist, whether the Quiet has its own intelligence, what the gate agent's memory loss means for memory in general.

### Design implications

- The game must reward all three layers. A character whose player is happy at the surface should not be mechanically punished for it.
- The deep-layer reveals should never require a specific PC to *get it* first. The party can include zero deep-layer engagers and the campaign still works.
- The DM should **never frame the deep layer as "the real game"**. The surface drama IS the real game. The deep stuff is texture for players who want it.
- A PC who is *characterologically* uninterested in science / philosophy / magic is a perfectly valid PC and a great anchor for the table. Their happy obliviousness is texture, not failure.

### When to nudge a layer

- A surface-engaged player who wants to stay surface-engaged: leave alone.
- A middle-engaged player who is starting to deep-engage: yes-and their questions; share campaign material they ask for.
- A deep-engaged player who has decided they've figured it out: hold the ambiguity. Ask *"what do you think it means?"* and listen.

A deep-engaged player who *insists* on confirming theories: see [`../../episodes/001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-gate.md`](../../episodes/001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-gate.md), the "off the record" handling.

### What this principle does NOT mean

It does not mean dumbing down for surface players. It does not mean keeping deep material out of the campaign. It does not mean refusing to engage with deep questions when players raise them.

It means: every layer is a complete game. A campaign run with five surface-engaged players is not a degraded campaign; it's a different campaign, equally good. A campaign run with five deep-engaged players is not the "right" campaign; it's a different campaign, equally good. The DM tunes the texture to the table.

## How these two principles interact

The *possible future, not prophecy* principle keeps players safe from feeling personally targeted by the campaign's events. The *engagement layers* principle keeps players safe from feeling obliged to chase the campaign's deep machinery. Together they say:

> *"Underleaf is for you. The world looks like your world; the events do not have to be your events. The cosmology is here; you do not have to read it. Have the kind of fun you want to have."*

These are the principles. Hold them when you DM.
