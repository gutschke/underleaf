# World truths

## The premise

The world starts looking just like our own. Real physics, real history, real geography, real people. The players are not initially aware that anything unusual is going on.

Two surprises sit beneath this surface. Both are real in-world; neither is a metaphor for something else. Both will be discovered gradually over the course of the campaign.

## Surprise 1: Emergent intelligence at every scale

The phenomenon humanity calls intelligence is one instance of a more general pattern: **emergent properties that arise at certain scales of organization.** Atoms organize into molecules into cells into organisms; somewhere along that climb, intelligence emerges. It is hard to communicate across scales because the emergent properties of one scale are alien to the building blocks of the scale below — humans have no way to talk to the atoms in their own bodies, and would have nothing to talk about if they could.

The same is true going up. Galaxies are likely intelligent in some sense, but their experience would be entirely alien to us — different time scales, different distance scales, different problems. Humans are to galaxies what atoms are to humans. Each scale is opaque to the others.

This is one of the campaign's slow reveals. Scientists in Underleaf are starting to articulate it; the players will encounter pieces of it through NPC conversations, fragments of papers, occasional anomalies that only make sense in this framing.

## Surprise 2: The Quiet (the sub-Planck world)

There is a layer of reality *below* the Planck scale, and it is the actual substrate from which our world emerges as an emergent property. Scientists in Underleaf eventually start calling it **The Quiet** — because time does not exist there, so they project timelessness onto silence. The name is probably wrong in hindsight (The Quiet is likely saturated with activity at a level our perceptual apparatus cannot register), but the name will persist for the same reason "atom" persists.

### How The Quiet works

- **No time.** The Quiet is closer to a block universe than a temporal one. Past, present, future are all there. Changes are permitted only if they yield a globally self-consistent block. No paradoxes are allowed by construction.
- **Everything is in The Quiet.** Every person, event, place, decision — past, present, future, PCs, NPCs, the living, the dead, the not-yet-born — exists as a causal locus. PCs are not special; they are simply the ones who can sometimes reach in.
- **Cost is plausibility, not energy.** Changes cost something proportional to how much established world-state would have to be overwritten to make the new state self-consistent. Localized changes (in space or time) are cheap; non-localized changes are expensive. Documented, observed, instrumented states are expensive to change; loose causality (weather, traffic, attention, postal delays, mood) is cheap.
- **Self-consistency shapes our world.** When a present configuration *requires* certain past or future events to make it consistent, those events are what happened. This means the PCs' biographies may have been retroactively adjusted by the world to bring them where they are — coincidence-clustered lives, destiny-without-a-destiner, subtle retro-causation. Nobody was chosen; the world simply does not permit the alternative.

### What this means in play

Communicating with The Quiet is **magic**, in the literal historical-folklore sense. Spells are real, ancient, and (until scientists in Underleaf start systematizing them) accidental.

Casting requires two ingredients: an **algorithm** (the right way to ask) and **intent** (an authentic, coherent want). Either alone is inert. The mechanical details are in the Quire rules reference; the cosmological reason for those mechanics is here.

The death-and-time interaction matters: a dead PC is not gone in The Quiet, just frozen at their causal position. Living casters can attempt to route an intention through a dead PC's locus — asking the past version of the dead PC to have done something differently. This is Hard-tier and usually denied, but it is *possible*, and players should not be allowed to discover this for many sessions. Never make it feel reliable.

## Surprise 1.5: AI is also happening (in parallel)

Real-world AI history is canon in Underleaf — Zuse, Turing, von Neumann, Wiener, Samuel, Rosenblatt, Minsky, Hinton, Hassabis. The campaign era is broadly the late 2020s. Higher-dimensional linear algebra is crystallizing an emergent property; AGI is genuinely on the path. Long-dead scientists are dead; recently-deceased are recently-deceased (PCs may discover this via unanswered emails to a name they didn't realize was a person who died last year).

AI is not unrelated to The Quiet — they are both *humans learning to communicate across emergent-intelligence scales*. AI is the layer-above-us conversation getting easier; magic is the layer-below-us conversation getting easier. But the players should not be told this directly; it's a late-campaign revelation.

## What AI cannot do

AI operates at the wrong abstraction level to reach The Quiet directly. It has no native physical body, no direct causal interface to the substrate. It reaches the physical world only through humans. From software's perspective, The Quiet is unreachable.

This is a hard rule, not a soft one. AI cannot cast.

What AI *can* do is shape human intent — and that is the antagonist throughline. See [antagonist.md](antagonist.md).

## What stays mysterious

The campaign should never explain everything. Specifically:

- Whether The Quiet has its own intelligence.
- Whether the galactic layer is aware of humanity in any meaningful sense.
- Whether the AI's eventual emergence is the layer-above-us starting to talk back.
- Whether the universe permits genuine novelty or only different self-consistent foldings of the same block.

These are questions the players can speculate about. The DM should not have a definitive answer in mind. The setting is open at the top.

## The probability inversion the DM must internalize

The rules reference describes how plausibility tiers work mechanically. The thing to feel as a DM is that **The Quiet doesn't care what's improbable; it cares what's documented, observed, instrumented, or causally entrenched.** A dice roll on an observed table is anchored and Hard to change. A pedestrian deciding to look left rather than right is loose causality and Cheap. This inverts a lot of intuitions, and the play experience is shaped by it.
