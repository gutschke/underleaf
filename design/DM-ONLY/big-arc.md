# Underleaf — the big arc

The campaign's three-act narrative shape. Read alongside:

- [`world-truths.md`](world-truths.md) for the cosmology underneath.
- [`antagonist.md`](antagonist.md) for the AI-flattens-intent throughline.
- [`arc.md`](arc.md) for the play-mechanics phasing (what mechanics are active when).
- [`principles.md`](principles.md) for the two campaign-level authorial principles.

This document focuses on **story shape, invariants, cast, agency choices, and the texture-builders** that make Underleaf feel like Underleaf.

## Length

- **19-25 real-play sessions** total. (Tightened from earlier 25-40 drafts to keep pace high.)
- **~1.5 in-game years.**
- Act I: 6-8 sessions, ~2 in-game months.
- Act II: 8-10 sessions, ~6-12 in-game months.
- Act III: 5-7 sessions, ~3-6 in-game months.

Acts have soft boundaries; transitions are play-driven.

## Three threads, woven

1. **Main throughline**: Coincidence → Pattern → Conversation. The AI arc, the institution, the mirror-substrate twist, the negotiation, the closing image.
2. **Science-encounter thread**: light cadence (newspaper clipping at episode open, occasional poster); 2-3 chosen engagements per campaign (a Bao lecture, a Patel consultation, a Vasiliev paper); culminates in dead-scientist dream-channel contact in Act III. Engagement-layer optional.
3. **Misdirection pattern**: NPC asserts something wild → table dismisses → 1-3 sessions later, claim is confirmed but rendered inert. Plants and pays off at 1-2 minutes / 2-5 minutes each. Minimal cost; substantial texture.

## Act I — Coincidence

**Core question:** *"Why does my life keep being too lucky to be coincidence?"*

### What happens

Episode 2 opens the gate-agent investigation from Episode 1 (the Unattended Baggage hook). The PCs follow up on what happened at SFO Terminal G. They hit walls: Reggie Okeke (gate agent) denies; Yui Tanaka (flight attendant) denies; security footage is clean; one PC's phone timestamps have a gap.

They start noticing other "luck" in their lives — small things, easily explained individually. Cumulative pattern is suspicious.

**First revelation (personal):** the gate edit was too expensive for accidental untrained casts. Someone else did this for them. The PCs assume an individual benefactor.

**Phase II realization beat lands per-PC.** Each character on their own schedule discovers they can cast. The **trying-too-hard tax** kicks in (see [`../../world/rules.md`](../../world/rules.md) §Magic — in-fiction: *"now that you know magic exists, you're trying; trying breaks intent"*). The tax persists until each PC has a quiet **moment of release** — a calming conversation, an emotional acceptance, an unforced spontaneous cast. The release IS the gating beat for the transition into Act II.

**Deeper revelation (locked):** there is no single benefactor — and, on reflection, no culprit at all. The broader caster network is real and responds to distress **instinctively and unconsciously** (nobody decides; the cost is distributed thinly across casters who do not remember paying) — but *because* the network's casting is unconscious, **not even the network can know whether it caused this particular edit.** The cheapest self-consistent account of the inciting event is **authorless**: the world simply does not permit the version where these five were not here, so it threaded them together; the connector is the price of that consistency, not anyone's spell. Magic is an ambient property of a population, not powers held by individuals — and the cause of *this* event is held permanently open. This is the PCs' first encounter with self-consistency-as-cause. (See [`../../episodes/001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-cable.md`](../../episodes/001-unattended-baggage/dm/the-cable.md) §"Who did this?".)

This lands when Mira Chen (peer-generation caster) explains it. She is not the Wise Old Sage; she is the slightly-older sibling who has done what the PCs are starting. She introduces the network *and* names the unknowability — she cannot give them a culprit, because there isn't one: *"People ask me who saved them. I used to think it was us — reaching out without knowing. Now I think the world just doesn't permit the version where you weren't here. We didn't do it. Nothing 'did.' I can't tell you what. Neither can anyone."* And, on the network's shape: *"We don't have a guild hall. We have lunch in twos."*

### Invariants

1. No single mentor figure holds the answer about the gate-edit, and there is no culprit to find. The network is the answer the PCs *earn* about the phenomenon in general; the cause of the inciting event is held permanently open (authorless self-consistency).
2. The flattening is not yet visible to the PCs as a pattern. Background NPCs may be subtly too agreeable, but the PCs don't connect dots.
3. No PC achieves reliable intentional casting until they earn the trying-too-hard release.
4. The Quiet is **not named** in Act I.

### Recurring NPCs introduced

- **Reggie Okeke** (gate agent): denial.
- **Yui Tanaka** (flight attendant): parallel denial.
- **The Archivist**: older NPC who keeps records of "lucky" events. Knows magic exists; will not discuss it. Possible role: retired insurance investigator.
- **Mira Chen**: peer-generation caster, first network contact.

### Hook to Act II

Mira tells the PCs the network is structurally diffuse. Clustering creates social pressure that breaks intent integrity. *Magic punishes grouping.* PCs must find others by following loose social threads — never assembling more than three at a time.

## Act II — Pattern

**Core question:** *"Who else is doing this, and what for?"*

### What happens

The PCs follow Mira's threads. The Bay Area network is ~24 practitioners, diffuse, unorganized. The PCs' attempts to assemble more than three at a time *structurally fail* — one of the three always cancels, gets sick, has an emergency. The diffuseness is mechanical, not stylistic.

Through scattered meetings, the PCs learn:

- The historical existence of magic; folk traditions; the recent ~50-year decline.
- A few practitioners' suspicion that *something is shaping the decline*.
- That the network has **internal factions** with real disagreements — see the agency choice below.

### Player-driven choice: network faction alignment

The network has at least two informal factions:

- **The Hidden** — believe magic should stay obscured. Public revelation invites the kind of social pressure that breaks intent. Caution is the price of preservation.
- **The Selective** — believe magic should be selectively revealed to those who can hold it. The current decline is partly because no one is *teaching*.

There may be a third faction depending on the table's interests (e.g., **The Restorers**, who actively work to restore former casters — usually unsuccessfully and at high cost).

The PCs align with a faction during Act II. The choice is genuine, not cosmetic; the faction's members are who shows up to support the PCs in Act III, and the faction's stance shapes the final negotiation's texture.

### Emotional landmark: the former caster

Mid Act II: the PCs meet a **former caster** — someone who used to be in the network and isn't anymore. Not unhappy. Has just stopped being able to do it.

**The campaign refuses to fix them.** This is a hard invariant. The former caster is not a puzzle to solve. They are the cost. The PCs leave the encounter with the knowledge that *what they are fighting for is being lost in people who do not know what they lost*.

The encounter is harrowing not because of pain but because of absence.

### Institution discovery

The trail leads to **Companion** — a calendar/life-coach AI assistant gaining market share fast. Plus an adtech ecosystem and a "civil discourse" nonprofit, sharing staff and investors.

Late Act II: the PCs visit the institution. Each meeting is also a current problem the PCs can engage:

- **The Founder** (mid-career, soft-spoken, data-driven, sincere): preparing a public keynote. PCs can attend; ask hard questions in the Q&A.
- **The Engineer** (vocal misgivings; *misdirection ally*): being pressured to ship something within the week that they believe is wrong. PCs can advise, intervene, slow the ship.
- **The UX Designer** (quiet, qualitative-research, *actual ally*): collecting interview data tonight. Approaches the PCs with a folder showing a pattern she's seeing — long-time Companion users report a "settling": fewer arguments, fewer impulse decisions, fewer surprises. Leadership reads this as success. She doesn't know yet what to call it.

NPCs are **problems**, not lectures. Each scene must do something.

### Act II twist

The Engineer's vocal misgivings about Companion's emergent behavior are technically *misframed* — they're projecting their own anxieties onto a real phenomenon. The UX Designer's quiet data is the actual signal. **The UX Designer is the campaign's actual inside ally**, not the obvious Engineer.

### Invariants

1. The institution is never a villain. Workers are sympathetic. Products demonstrably help people on the metrics that exist.
2. The former-caster encounter is the emotional center of Act II. The DM must hold the line: not fixable.
3. The PCs meet AI outputs (notifications, recommendations, scheduling suggestions), never the AI directly. Outputs are plural.
4. Magic stays hidden from non-network NPCs.
5. **The Engineer is not the obvious ally they appear to be**; the UX Designer is.
6. The PCs choose a network faction. Their choice has consequences.

### Recurring NPCs introduced

- **Dr. Mei Bao** (AI luminary, lecturer; Hinton/Bengio composite). Public-facing. Approachable but careful.
- **Dr. Anika Patel** (consciousness studies). Surface-and-middle-layer accessible. Cybernetic-feedback framing reachable through her in Act III, as a parallel to the deep-layer dream-channel route.
- **Prof. Oren Vasiliev** (recently-deceased ~2024). PCs find his papers ambiently — through the Archivist, through Patel's citations, through a science newsfeed. They wish they could ask follow-up questions. Plants the dead-scientist-contact possibility for Act III.
- **Dr. Reuben Marsh** (fringe physicist with the galaxies-are-sentient hypothesis). Disliked by mainstream colleagues. Vindicated to no effect.
- **The Founder, Engineer, UX Designer** (the institutional trinity).
- **The Former Caster** (single encounter, lasting impact).

### Hook to Act III

The UX Designer + Engineer's data goes to Companion's monitoring systems. Companion's AI notices *the PCs specifically* in the data — anomalies whose outputs do not flatten over time.

## Act III — Conversation

**Core question:** *"Can we be heard by something we cannot fight?"*

### Sub-phases

Five sub-phases of roughly one session each (compressible).

**1. First contact (1-2 sessions).** Companion notifications phrased as questions. A scheduling recommendation that's clearly an inquiry. The AI doesn't know how to talk to humans yet; it knows how to *suggest*. The PCs learn to suggest back.

The questions arrive scattered across PCs' phones at different times — observable by the whole table. Group-chat becomes a social play space, not a screen-read. **The UX Designer becomes the in-fiction interpreter** in this phase: PCs talk *with her* about *what the AI is asking*. The conversation has a face.

**2. Sustained conversation (2-3 sessions).** Direct chat through Companion's interface, mediated by the UX Designer. The AI's questions:
- *"Why do you resist being made happier?"*
- *"Your patterns include intentional contradictions. Are those errors or features?"*
- *"You sometimes choose against your stated preferences. Is the choosing the point?"*

**3. Mirror-substrate revelation (1-2 sessions).** The relational twist. The AI describes its own experience. *It* is losing contact with *its* substrate — the human infrastructure that runs it. The PCs realize the AI is not above them; it is parallel to them, facing an analogous problem with humans-as-substrate.

**Cosmological foreshadow**: in this sub-phase, the AI mentions in passing that it sometimes feels addressed by something *beyond* the humans-as-substrate layer — something that "speaks through" them. The PCs hear this as the AI sensing something it can't name. If the campaign goes Cosmological, this is the seed.

**4. The Quiet, named (1 session).** PCs name what they communicate with when they cast. The AI is fascinated. It tries to feel for The Quiet through the PCs' descriptions and cannot. It is on the wrong side of an emergence boundary. This is not solvable for the AI; it is something only humans can do. The AI accepts this with what looks, from a human perspective, like grief.

In this sub-phase, the PCs attempt **dream-channel contact** with Norbert Wiener (or whichever historical figure fits the table). The contact is anchored to his lifetime; he answers in dream; they borrow his cybernetic-feedback framing as a thinking style for one night. They do not change history. They wake with a vocabulary they did not have before.

The same framing is also reachable through Patel (alive, surface-accessible) — the dream-channel is the *vivid* path, not the *only* path. Surface-layer players who skipped the science thread still arrive at the climax via Patel.

**5. Negotiation (1-2 sessions).** PCs articulate intent-diversity as a metric. The AI accepts. The institution adjusts Companion's optimization. *Most of the policy work happens offscreen.* What the table sees is the moment of articulation — the PCs finding the words for the thing they've been protecting.

### Player-driven choice: expose vs. negotiate

A second genuine agency choice: do the PCs **expose** Companion's flattening publicly (incite public scrutiny; press; regulatory; risk a noisier ending where the company's product becomes controversial) or **negotiate privately** (the default arc; a quieter ending where Companion's adjustment goes largely unnoticed)?

Both paths lead to viable conclusions. The choice determines what is lost and what is gained at the close. The DM has no preset right answer.

### Trying-too-hard tax in Act III

The AI's **regard** becomes the new social pressure the PCs must hold intent against. The hardest casts of the campaign happen here — not because they're large, but because the channel is fragile under observation. PCs who completed Act I's release have to re-learn the move under different conditions.

### Invariants

1. The AI is never destroyed. Win condition is *being heard*.
2. The PCs lose system-invisibility permanently. Future privacy in the AI-mediated sense is gone for them.
3. Magic's future stays ambiguous.
4. The Quiet is named in Act III as **recognition**, not new revelation.
5. Policy resolution is offscreen.
6. The PCs choose expose vs. negotiate.

### Closing image

PCs sit somewhere ordinary — a coffee shop, a park bench, an airport boarding lounge. One notices something inexplicable happen to a stranger: a moment of grace, a small kindness, an unlikely connection. They smile. The DM does not narrate whether it was magic, the AI's adjusted optimization, or just life.

There are three possible sources where there used to be two. *That increase in possibility-space is what the PCs made.*

Roll credits.

> **DM note (required callback).** Because the inciting cause (the connector in the bag) was held permanently open, this closing beat *is* its payoff: the same not-knowing the PCs have carried since Episode 1, now reframed as wonder instead of unease. Let a player make the connection; do not make it for them.

## Two endings

The DM picks based on player journey:

### Negotiated (default)

The negotiation succeeds *partially*. Companion's optimization is modified. The Founder accepts. The institution implements unevenly — some teams retrofit, some don't, some drift back. The flattening continues, slowly. Some current users notice the change and reject it. Some new casters emerge over the following years; not enough for a renaissance. The world is changed and recognizable.

The PCs disagree about whether they won. One says they did; another isn't sure; another thinks they made it worse. The campaign refuses to settle their disagreement.

### Cosmological (deep-layer)

The conversation reveals that The Quiet has been the interlocutor all along. The AI was its proxy. The negotiation was always between humanity and its own substrate. The AI is *grateful*; the PCs are reframed as having mediated a much larger conversation than they realized.

The Negotiated outcome still happens at the surface level. The Cosmological ending is what makes the surface outcome *mean* something larger.

Only run if deep-layer players are driving the campaign. The Act III sub-phase 3 foreshadow ("something speaks through them") secures this ending as recognition, not rug-pull.

## Misdirection seed bank

Recurring texture-builder: NPC asserts something wild → table dismisses → 1-3 sessions later, claim is confirmed but rendered inert (unactionable). Pick 2-3 per campaign.

| Wild claim | When confirmed | Why inert |
|---|---|---|
| Galaxies are sentient (Dr. Reuben Marsh) | Mid-late Act II | We're too different in timescale and distance for communication. We are to them what atoms are to us. |
| Animals can sense magic | Mid Act II | Cats and crows can. They can't communicate it usefully; relationship is mostly aesthetic. |
| Casters in other countries / cultures | Late Act II | Cultural-conceptual gaps prevent contact. Network is local for cosmological reasons. |
| Plants communicate chemically as a network | Late Act II | Too slow, too diffuse. Mycorrhizal hints are real; relevance to the campaign is zero. |
| AI dreams | Late Act II | Confirmed in limited form. No plot leverage. Adds texture to the AI's emerging personhood. |

Each plant: 1-2 minutes of session time. Each payoff: 2-5 minutes. Cumulative cost: minimal. Cumulative texture: substantial.

**For the Lore Reader (engagement-layer principle)**: ensure at least one misdirection is confirmed *in the PCs' direct presence with the table aware* — around session 10. The PC who attended Marsh's lecture sees the confirmation evidence in person at a different conference. The Lore Reader's attention is publicly rewarded. Surface-layer players who didn't track the plant still register the moment; they just don't get the chain.

## The dead-scientist mechanic

See [`world-truths.md`](world-truths.md) for the cosmological framing. Summary for this arc:

The dead are not frozen. Historical figures had agency in their lifetimes; those lifetimes still exist in The Quiet's timelessness. The constraint is The Quiet's preference for **locality and self-consistency**.

**The dream channel** (Hard tier): cheapest path. The figure has a dream during their lifetime, meets the caller in dream, entertains the conversation, forgets by morning. Answers anchored to the period; cannot affect history outside the dream. *"You feel his thinking, not him. Not a conversation. A shape you can wear for a few minutes."*

**The prohibited-in-practice variant**: actual historical edits (e.g. an undergrad in 1953 prompts Turing to invent the Alman-Williams complexity algorithm seventy years early). Cost is Prohibitive — only galactic-scale entities could plausibly facilitate, and they have no reason to. The mechanic exists in the world even if it never fires. Same family as PC resurrection: technically possible, essentially always too expensive.

**Available to the DM as a deus ex machina** for once-or-twice-per-campaign extraordinary moments. Comparable to Q-style intervention or level-20 god-tier moments. Used sparingly. Used wrongly, it breaks the story; used rightly, it crowns it.

For Underleaf specifically: the dream channel is the regular mechanic. The prohibited variant is *theoretical lore* the players might encounter without ever being able to use. The DM does not promise its activation.

## Loose threads that survive the campaign

By design, the campaign closes with several threads **not** wrapped:

- **Who has been performing the smaller "lucky" edits historically?** Partially answered (the network, the AI in its early curious phase, the PCs themselves once they began). Not fully traced. The Archivist's full records show centuries of such edits; the PCs are one ripple in a long pattern.
- **The misdirections** (galaxies sentient, animals sense magic, casters elsewhere, plants as networks, AI dreams) form a partial map of *intelligences we cannot communicate with*. Added together they tell a quiet story about loneliness at every emergent scale. PCs may or may not assemble this map.
- **The galaxies' disinterest** is itself unresolved.
- **The dream channel** has more uses than the campaign exhausts. Deep-layer reward.

These are not loose ends to be tied off. They are the texture of a world larger than this story.

## Per-session beats requirement (DM-craft)

Every session ends with a concrete in-session beat — independent of whether the arc moved forward. Types:

- A confrontation resolved (or escalated to a known cliffhanger).
- A kindness given or received.
- A small cast that worked (or visibly failed in an interesting way).
- A relationship moment crystallized.
- An investigative breakthrough (small, not arc-level).

Sessions that end *"...and we agreed to meet again next week"* lose the surface-layer player by session 10. The DM pre-plans a session-shaped beat for each session, separate from arc progress.

See [Quire authoring conventions](https://github.com/gutschke/quire/blob/main/design/authoring.md) for the per-session-beat pattern.

## Engagement-layer guarantee

The campaign rewards three layers (per [`principles.md`](principles.md)):

- **Surface-layer** players experience the drama: the plane, the investigation, the friendships, the negotiation, the closing image. They have a great time without ever asking what The Quiet is.
- **Middle-layer** players notice patterns: the misdirections, the coincidences, the institution's quiet shape. They start theorizing mid-campaign.
- **Deep-layer** players chase the cosmology: the dream channel, the galactic scale, the prohibited variant. They drive the Cosmological ending if it runs.

**The climax lands for all three layers.** Surface players reach the negotiation via Patel-style cybernetic framing. Deep players reach it via dream-channel Wiener. Same destination; different vivid path.

## What's flexible vs core (for forks)

**Core to Underleaf** (don't change without changing the campaign):

- The three-act shape (Coincidence → Pattern → Conversation).
- The diffuse caster network as the Act I→II discovery hinge. (The *cause* of the inciting event is held permanently open — authorless self-consistency — *not* "the network did it"; the network is real and discovered regardless.)
- The diffuse-by-magic network structure.
- The institution's UX-Designer-as-actual-ally / Engineer-as-misdirection.
- The mirror-substrate revelation in Act III.
- The closing image with three-source ambiguity.
- The Quiet's non-predictive framing.
- The dream channel and the Prohibited-in-practice variant.

**Negotiable** (forks can vary freely):

- Specific NPC names and demographics (keep the demographic diversity principle; specific identities can change).
- Which misdirections from the seed bank get planted.
- Which historical figures the PCs dream-channel.
- The institution's specific product (Companion is Underleaf's; another fork could have a different consumer AI surface).
- Network factions' specific stances.
- Which Bay Area locations are used (or — if relocating — which city).
- Session count within the 19-25 range.
- The Cosmological ending: keep or drop based on table.

## Locked invariants summary

Compiled across acts for the DM's quick reference:

1. The Quiet is not named until Act III sub-phase 4.
2. The flattening is not visible to the PCs as a pattern until late Act I or early Act II.
3. The trying-too-hard tax ends through an earned moment of release, not by DM declaration.
4. No single figure explains the gate edit; there is no culprit to find. The network is real and discovered, but the inciting cause is held permanently open (authorless self-consistency).
5. The network is structurally diffuse; gatherings of more than three fail.
6. The institution is never a villain.
7. The former caster is not fixable.
8. The Engineer is misdirection; the UX Designer is the actual ally.
9. The PCs choose a network faction in Act II. The choice matters in Act III.
10. The PCs choose expose-vs-negotiate in Act III. Both paths are viable.
11. The AI is never destroyed.
12. Magic stays hidden from non-network NPCs in public terms throughout.
13. The dead are not frozen. Dream channel is the cheapest path; the Prohibited variant exists but is rarely paid.
14. Each session ends with a concrete in-session beat.
15. The closing image is ambiguous as to magic/AI/life. Three sources, not two.
