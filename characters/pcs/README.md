# Player characters

This folder is for *your* characters. Players edit freely. The Quire runtime gives you an in-browser sheet — you should rarely need to touch the files directly.

## Before you create a character

Underleaf has a guiding principle that the campaign assumes from the start, so read this once:

**Your PC is not you.** Different name. Different gender if you want. Different identity. The PC lives in a world that looks a lot like yours — real Bay Area neighborhoods, real cafés, real freeways — but the PC is an alternate-life person who happens to live in places you know. This makes the eeriness land harder (the locations feel home) while keeping you out of the way when bad things happen to your character.

Underleaf is a *possible* future for our shared world, not a prophecy about anyone at the table. Use a different name. Pick pronouns and an identity that aren't yours. The setting can be home; the person living in it is not you.

## Creating a character

The PC schema is documented in [Quire's schema docs](https://github.com/gutschke/quire/tree/main/schema/v0). At minimum a PC needs a `name`; everything else is optional. A starting PC typically has:

- A name and pronouns.
- An alignment (one of nine — Lawful/Neutral/Chaotic crossed with Good/Neutral/Evil). Binding, but it can evolve through play.
- A stat block (STR/DEX/CON/INT/WIS/CHA, default array: one +2, three +1s, two 0s).
- A skill category they're good at, plus 3-5 free-text tags from their background.
- A backstory in Markdown.

## The mandatory question

There is one specific question your backstory should answer, even if obliquely:

> **What in your life taught you to hold an intention against pressure?**

It might be a quiet thing — a parent who taught you to think before agreeing, a year of homesickness that made you find what you actually wanted, a job where standing firm mattered. It might be loud — adversity, betrayal, an experience of being right when everyone else was wrong. It doesn't have to be dramatic. It does have to be specific.

You will not know why this question matters at the start. Trust that it does.

## Five more things to decide

When you make your PC, also pick:

1. **A Bay Area home.** Real neighborhood. Real coffee shops. Real walking routes. Or — *recent transplant*: "moved to SF six months ago for [job / family / love / restart]." The recent-transplant option works especially well because the unfamiliarity is built in.
2. **A personally meaningful item.** Could be anything — a piece of jewelry, a worn paperback, a specific code snippet, a phrase a grandparent used. You don't need to know yet why it matters.
3. **A vague prior connection** to at least one other PC. Don't coordinate the specifics with the other players at the table — let the DM line things up. Just describe a kind of person you might have brushed past in your life (a barista at a specific shop, an attendee at a specific event, a friend-of-a-friend).
4. **One reason you're on Flight 887 to Taipei.** Family emergency, work, wedding, funeral, visa appointment, vacation, recent breakup, whatever fits. At least one PC's reason should have crystallized in the last 72 hours — sudden phone call, sudden plan.
5. **Whether your character is interested in science / technology / philosophy.** Some PCs are deeply nerdy and chase every interesting idea. Some PCs notice technology the way they notice weather — it's there, it's fine. *Both kinds of PC have a great time in Underleaf.* Don't feel obliged to be the curious one.

## Archetypes

See [`archetypes.md`](archetypes.md) for five suggested PC shapes — Hacker, Engineer, Caregiver, Outsider, Operator. These are inspiration, not classes. You can ignore them entirely, mix them, or invent something completely different.

## In-medias-res

Underleaf episodes typically open with the PCs already in a scene together, not converging on one. You will not be asked to explain how you all met before play starts. Your backstory exists; your party formation will emerge through play. This is intentional and load-bearing.
