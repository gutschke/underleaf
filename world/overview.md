# Underleaf — the world as you know it

You live in our world, more or less.

It is the present day, give or take a year. You have a phone, a laptop, an email account, a fediverse handle, a few group chats, a job or school or a thing you're trying to make work. The news is loud and the news is always partly wrong. People talk about AI as if it were either going to save them or eat them, and you've stopped trying to figure out which camp is right.

The cities feel familiar. So do the apartments, the coffee, the bus routes, the late-night noodles. The big landmarks of the past century are the ones you'd expect: the world wars, the cold war, the personal computer, the internet, the smartphone, the gradual realization that climate change is no longer a forecast. Real history. Real physicists, real biologists, real computer scientists — most of them dead, some of them quite recent, a few still active and a little surprising in interviews.

You know that AI has been a research topic for a long time — eighty years, give or take — but most of that history happened in obscure labs at MIT and Stanford and Toronto, and most people never thought about it. The current chat bots are useful or annoying depending on the day. Some of your friends use them constantly. Some refuse on principle. The discourse is exhausting.

You are not particularly important in any of this. You are a person trying to live your life in an interesting time.

That is the world as you know it.
